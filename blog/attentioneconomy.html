The Attention Economy

May 10, 2026

There is a sentence that gets repeated a lot online: "If you're not paying for the product, you are the product." It's catchy, but it's not quite accurate. You aren't the product. Your attention is. And unlike most resources, attention is finite in a way that makes it extremely valuable — there are only so many hours in a day, and every company with a screen wants as many of them as possible.

What the attention economy actually is

The term was coined decades before smartphones existed, but the idea is simple: in a world overflowing with information, the scarce resource isn't content — it's the human capacity to absorb it. Businesses figured out that whoever captures the most attention can sell that attention to advertisers. The more time you spend on a platform, the more ads you see, and the more money the platform makes. Your engagement is the revenue model.

This sounds abstract until you realize it explains almost every design decision in modern apps. Why does Instagram have no chronological feed? Because a curated feed, tuned to provoke a reaction, keeps you scrolling longer than seeing posts in order would. Why do notifications arrive as red badges? Because red signals urgency, and urgency makes you tap. None of this is accidental. It is the output of teams of engineers and behavioral psychologists whose job is to extract more time from you.

Outrage is efficient fuel

The attention economy doesn't just want any attention — it wants emotional attention, because emotion is what drives engagement. And the emotion that drives the most engagement, consistently, is outrage. An infuriating post gets shared far more than a pleasant one. A scary headline gets clicked far more than a reassuring one.

This is why feeds filled with content designed to provoke a strong reaction are so common. It's not that the platforms endorse that content — they're largely indifferent to it. What they care about is that you reacted, that you stayed, that you came back. Outrage keeps the engine running. The psychological cost of that — the low-grade anxiety, the feeling of the world being worse than it is — falls entirely on you.

The cost is invisible

The reason this is so hard to push back against is that the cost doesn't show up anywhere obvious. You don't get a bill at the end of the month listing the hours you lost to mindless scrolling. You don't receive a summary of how many times your mood was manipulated by an algorithmically selected post. The extraction is quiet and continuous, which is exactly what makes it effective.

Over time, though, the effects compound. Shortened attention spans. A reflexive need to reach for a phone during any moment of stillness. Difficulty focusing on anything that doesn't deliver instant feedback. These aren't personality flaws — they're the predictable result of spending years inside systems engineered to rewire your behavior.

Knowing is not enough

I want to be honest about something: understanding the attention economy doesn't make you immune to it. I know exactly how infinite scroll works, why notifications are designed to feel urgent, and what behavioral loops these platforms are exploiting — and I still feel the pull. Awareness is a starting point, not a solution.

What actually helps is friction. Making the apps harder to reach. Removing them from the home screen. Turning off notifications. Logging out so that opening the app requires a deliberate step. These aren't elegant fixes, but they work because they interrupt the automatic behavior before it starts. The goal isn't willpower — it's restructuring the environment so that willpower doesn't have to do all the work.

Who wins when you scroll

It's worth asking, plainly, who benefits from the way you currently spend your time online. Not in a dramatic way — just as a practical question. When you open an app out of habit and spend twenty minutes on it, what did you get out of it, and what did the platform get out of it? In most cases, the exchange is lopsided in a way that becomes obvious once you look at it directly.

Your attention is one of the few things that is genuinely, irreversibly yours. Once you spend an hour, it's gone. The companies competing for it have billions of dollars and decades of research on their side. The least you can do is make them work for it.
